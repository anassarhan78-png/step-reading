Passage 16 · The Importance of Mangroves

🌿 Guardians of the Coast

Mangroves are unique trees and shrubs that grow in salty coastal waters, mainly in tropical and subtropical regions. With their tangled roots rising above the water, they form dense forests that serve as a vital link between land and sea. Despite covering less than 1% of tropical forests worldwide, mangroves provide immense ecological and economic benefits.

One of their most critical roles is coastal protection. The dense root systems act as natural barriers, absorbing wave energy and reducing erosion from storms and tsunamis. They also trap sediment, which helps maintain water quality and allows the coastline to build up naturally. Furthermore, mangroves are extraordinary carbon sinks; they store up to four times more carbon per hectare than most other forests, making them essential in the fight against climate change.

Mangrove forests are biodiversity hotspots. They provide nursery habitats for fish, crabs, and shrimp, supporting commercial fisheries that millions of people depend on. Birds, monkeys, and even endangered species like the Bengal tiger find refuge in these forests. Local communities rely on mangroves for timber, fuel, and medicinal plants.

Sadly, mangroves are disappearing at an alarming rate due to human activities. Clearing for shrimp farming, coastal development, and pollution have destroyed nearly half of the world's mangroves in the past fifty years. Recognizing their value, conservation groups and governments are now working to restore degraded areas and establish protected zones. Community‑led planting projects and sustainable aquaculture practices offer hope. Protecting mangroves means safeguarding coastlines, biodiversity, and livelihoods for future generations.
